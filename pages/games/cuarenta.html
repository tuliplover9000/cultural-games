Cuarenta

Ecuador  ·  Card

🃏 This game uses virtual cards only. No real money is involved.
How to Play ▼

Cuarenta uses a 40-card Spanish deck (suits: Oros, Copas, Espadas, Bastos; ranks: As, 2–7, Sota, Caballo, Rey — note 8 and 9 are excluded). First player to reach 40 points wins.

  1. Setup: 5 cards are placed face-up on the mesa (table). Each player receives 10 cards.
  2. Your turn: Click a card in your hand to select it (it lifts up). Then click anywhere in the mesa zone to play it.
  3. Captures:
    • Secuencia (sequence): if your card + 2 or more consecutive table cards form a run of 3+, you capture them all. Rank order: As–2–3–4–5–6–7–Sota–Caballo–Rey (7 and Sota are adjacent).
    • Pares (pairs): if your card matches the rank of one or more table cards, you capture all matching cards.
    • Sequences take priority over pairs.
  4. No capture: if neither applies, your card is added to the mesa.
  5. ¡Caída! +1 bonus: if you capture immediately after your opponent captured, and you play the same rank they played — that's a Caída!
  6. ¡Mesa! +1 bonus: clearing all cards from the mesa earns one Mesa point.
  7. Scoring per ronda:
    • Each As captured: 1 pt (max 4)
    • 7 de Oros: 1 pt
    • Sota de Oros: 1 pt
    • Each Mesa: 1 pt
    • Most cards captured: 1 pt (tie = 0)
  8. When both hands are empty, deal 10 more cards each. When the deck runs out, score the ronda and begin a new one. First to 40 pts total wins.
Cultural Context ▼

Cuarenta is Ecuador's national card game, played enthusiastically across the country from Quito's urban cafés to rural communities in the Sierra and Costa. The name simply means "forty" — the target score and the deck size, both rolled into one. It is the card game Ecuadorians grow up with, the one played at family gatherings, political meetings, and long afternoons in any tienda with a deck of Spanish cards.

The game is played with a baraja española (Spanish-suited deck), a colonial inheritance that arrived with conquistadors in the 16th century and was thoroughly adopted into Andean culture. Over four centuries the Ecuadorian variant evolved its own rules — particularly the Caída mechanic and the 40-point scoring target — creating a game that feels distinctly local even as it shares ancestry with Spanish games like Brisca and Scopa.

Cuarenta is serious business: national tournaments draw crowds, and skilled players command real respect. The Caída — that moment when you counter your opponent's rank immediately after they capture — is celebrated like a chess fork, a flash of timing and anticipation that separates good players from great ones.
