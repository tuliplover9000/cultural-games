Oware

West Africa — Board

How to Play ▼

Oware is played on a board with 2 rows of 6 pits and a score pit on each end. Each pit starts with 4 seeds (48 total).

  1. Click one of your 6 pits to sow. Seeds are picked up and distributed one per pit, counterclockwise.
  2. Capture: if the last seed lands in an opponent's pit bringing it to exactly 2 or 3 seeds, you capture those seeds. Continue capturing backwards as long as pits have exactly 2 or 3 seeds.
  3. Starvation rule: you may not make a move that leaves your opponent with zero seeds — unless it is your only possible move.
  4. The game ends when one player has 25+ seeds captured, or fewer than 12 seeds remain on the board.
  5. The player who captured the most seeds wins.
Cultural Context ▼

Oware — also spelled Owarè — is one of the oldest known board games in the world, originating among the Akan people of present-day Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire. Archaeological evidence suggests mancala-family games have been played in Africa for over 7,000 years.

The game's name comes from the Twi language and is often translated as "he/she marries." It is deeply embedded in Akan culture, where it is played at social gatherings, during festivals, and as a rite of passage for young people. Oware boards — beautifully carved from hardwood — are cherished family heirlooms. The game spread throughout the African diaspora via the transatlantic slave trade, with variants now found across the Caribbean and parts of South America.
